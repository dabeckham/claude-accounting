I Finally Hit the Wall on Max5 — and I Was Wrong About Why

By Don Beckham · 1 July 2026

For weeks I'd said I had never once hit the 5-hour limit on the $100/month Max5 plan. Last night I finally did. I had an immediate hunch about the cause — and it was mostly wrong. Chasing down the real reason turned my own ledger into a clean falsification test of the single assumption every one of these write-ups rests on: that cached tokens are free against the meter. Here's the wall, the wrong guess, and the proof.

TL;DR

  • First time hitting the 5-hour wall on Max5 ($100/mo). Anthropic doubled these limits in May, so it takes serious volume — the window opened ~8:20 PM, I exhausted it by ~12:20 AM, locked out until it reset at 1:20 AM.
  • My first guess — that thinking re-emission, which I'd left ON in one project, filled the window and caused it — was mostly wrong. The wall is driven by output, and I was running four output-heavy autonomous agents at once.
  • The real prize: the ledger let me falsify the cache question directly. One 5-hour window sailed through 529 million cache tokens with no throttle; a later one walled at half that cache but more fresh in+out. Cache's weight against the limit is under 0.24% of the input rate — effectively zero.
  • The meter counts what the model freshly ingests and generates. Cache — the biggest line on your bill — is free against the rate limit. Now proven from my own data, not assumed.

1. The wall I'd been told I'd never hit

The earlier limits piece worked the caps out indirectly and noted that on Max5 I'd never actually reached the 5-hour ceiling — my heaviest windows topped out around two-thirds of it. Anthropic had just doubled Claude Code's 5-hour limits (6 May) and lifted weekly limits 50% (13 May, through 13 July), so there was a lot of headroom. Hitting the wall on that plan takes real work.

Last night I did. Mid-session, the model simply stopped: the 5-hour budget was spent.

5-hour limit100%
Weekly · all models11%

Window opened ~8:20 PM (30 Jun); exhausted ~12:20 AM; reset 1:20 AM. The ledger confirms it: the last turn that completed is stamped 12:16 AM, and the next prompt — 12:24 AM — has no matching completion. That dangling prompt is the throttle.

So the window didn't run out of time; it ran out of budget, about four hours in. The obvious question: what did I do differently that finally pushed me over?

2. My first guess — and why the data overruled it

I had an immediate suspect. In one of the projects — call it Jarvis — I'd turned on thinking re-emission: every reasoning block gets copied into the visible output. I'd literally watched the 5-hour slider crawl while it worked, and I knew from the cost write-up that re-emission fills the context window fast. Case closed, surely: re-emission burned the window, turn it off, problem solved.

Except the ledger didn't back me up. Breaking the wall window down by project, by the tokens that actually count against the limit (fresh input + output):

ProjectOutput tokensOut / turnShare of counted tokens
Revere642,27242,81832.2%
claude_code_frigate596,12737,25727.9%
Jarvis (re-emission ON)467,91538,99224.3%
frigate-revere-produ322,703107,56715.7%

Jarvis was third — behind two projects with re-emission off and comparable output per turn. I wasn't felled by one setting; I was running four output-heavy autonomous agents in parallel for four hours. The decisive check: if I zero out all of Jarvis's output, the other three still sum to 1.78M — 79% of a maxed window on their own. Re-emission trims only part of one project's share; it was never going to be the difference between hitting the wall and not.

The subtler error in my hunch was a meter mix-up. Re-emission fills the context window — that's real, and it's what I watched. But a full context is cache, and (as we're about to prove) cache is free against the 5-hour limit. The thing I watched fill fast is not the thing that hit the wall. Re-emission costs you dollars and compaction headroom; it barely touches the rate meter. Two different meters.

Which is the whole ethos of this project turned on myself: test, don't theorize. The tidy story was wrong, and the data said so in about thirty seconds.

3. The assumption worth testing: is cache really free?

That left the real driver — output — and one nagging thread. Every piece here has leaned on a claim I'd only ever asserted: that the rate limit counts fresh input and output, while cache reads and writes are free. If that's even slightly wrong, my whole model of the meter is wrong. Hitting the wall gave me the two data points to settle it, so I stopped asserting and tested.

The logic of the test: if cache counted against the limit at any meaningful weight, then my cache-heavy history — hundreds of millions of cache tokens per five-hour stretch — would have walled me long before last night. It never had. Time to make that quantitative.

4. The two-window proof

The cleanest evidence is two five-hour windows on the same machine — so there's nothing to argue about — where one sailed through and the other walled:

5-hour windowFresh in+outCache (read+write)Outcome
ending 30 Jun 5:40 PM1,610,315528,901,202✓ no throttle
ending 1 Jul ~12:20 AM (the wall)2,246,747264,143,040✗ throttled

Read the ordering, because it's the whole argument:

  • The window I sailed through had twice the cache (529M vs 264M). If cache counted, that window would have drained the budget faster and walled me first. It didn't.
  • The window that walled me had more fresh in+out (2.25M vs 1.61M). The metric that correctly ordered “survived below, walled above” is in+out. Cache ordered the two backwards.

Turn that into a bound. For the survived window to sit under the ceiling the wall defines, the weight w the limit places on a cache token (relative to an input token) can be at most:

w < (2,246,747 − 1,610,315) / (528,901,202 − 264,143,040)
  = 636,432 / 264,758,162
  = 0.0024   →  0.24% of the input rate

Anthropic bills a cache read at 10% of the input rate. The rate limit weighs it at under 0.24% — more than 42× below even the discounted billing rate, and statistically indistinguishable from zero. Cache read and write are free against the 5-hour limit. Not assumed — falsified from my own ledger.

Put plainly: I pushed 529 million cache tokens through the model in five hours without moving the needle, then hit the wall on 2.25 million of fresh output and input. The meter charges for what the model generates and freshly reads, and waves through everything it re-reads from cache — which, on a long agentic session, is the overwhelming majority of the traffic.

5. So what actually moved the meter

Output did. In the wall window, 90% of the counted tokens were output (2.03M of 2.25M). Meanwhile 264M of cache rode along for free. That's the same lesson as the accounting walk-through and the limits piece, now demonstrated the hard way: what throttles you is generation, run across enough concurrent agents to pile up a couple million output tokens in five hours.

And it re-frames re-emission correctly. Since cache is free against this meter, re-emission's main effect — growing the cached context — costs you money and compaction headroom, not rate-limit budget. Worth turning off for those reasons; not the lever that keeps you under the 5-hour wall. My instinct pointed at the right kind of cost and the wrong meter.

6. The honest edges

Two caveats keep this from over-claiming.

My first, worse guess was that the limits had been cut

Before I suspected re-emission, my very first reflex was to reverse-engineer a ceiling and compare it to June — and the arithmetic looked like the caps had been halved. That's flatly contradicted by the record: Anthropic raised these limits (doubled the 5-hour in May, +50% weekly through July). When a number I derive disagrees with the vendor's published change in the opposite direction, the number is what I should distrust. The limits went up; the extra load was on my side.

This ledger sees one machine; the cap is account-wide

The token totals here come from Claude Code hooks on this machine, but the 5-hour and weekly caps bill across the whole account. So every ceiling I extract is a lower bound — “this machine spent at least 2.25M before the shared meter hit full,” not “the ceiling is exactly 2.25M.” Happily, the cache proof above is immune to this: both windows are the same machine's own traffic, so the “free” result stands regardless of what ran elsewhere. The ceiling number is the soft part; the cache-is-free finding is the solid one. Pinning the exact ceiling is a job for a clean window measured against the on-screen meter — the next experiment.

The headline, then, isn't a number. It's a correction and a confirmation: I hit the wall by generating too much across too many agents at once — not by any one clever setting — and in chasing the wrong culprit I finally proved the thing I'd been taking on faith. Cache is free. Output is the bill. Test, don't theorize — especially when the theory is your own.
