Now Let's Talk About Limits

By Don Beckham · 18 June 2026

A companion to the token-accounting write-up. The dollar cost of a session is one thing; the rate limit is another — a separate meter with its own rules. Here's what Claude's 5-hour and weekly limits actually count, reverse-engineered from my own usage, and why a giant cached context barely touches them.

TL;DR

  • There are two meters: a 5-hour rolling window and a weekly cap. They're separate from billing entirely.
  • They count only fresh input + output tokens. Cache reads and cache writes are essentially free against the limit — the opposite of what you'd guess.
  • Reverse-engineered ceilings (input+output tokens): $20 plan ≈ 660K / 5h; Max5 ≈ 3.6M / 5h (≈ 5×); Max5 weekly ≈ 43M.
  • So the weekly cap (~12 maxed 5-hour windows), not the 5-hour one, is the real ceiling for sustained work — and output volume is what burns it, not big context.
  • Consequence: a user who lives at the weekly cap costs Anthropic an estimated $1,000–5,000/month in compute against the $100 they pay — because the cap throttles throughput, not the cache cost underneath.

1. Two bars, and what they look like

Claude Code shows two usage meters. Right now, mid-morning, mine read:

5-hour limit5%
Weekly · all models16%

5-hour resets 10:39 AM · weekly resets in five days.

The 5-hour window is rolling: it starts with your first message and resets five hours later (mine began at 5:39 AM). The weekly cap spans seven days across all models. Both are about throughput, not money — you can be nowhere near your dollar-equivalent value and still hit a wall, or vice-versa.

2. The surprise: cache traffic is free against the limit

The instinct is that everything you send counts. It doesn't. At the moment that 5-hour bar read 5%, here's what my window had actually consumed:

input + output tokens (fresh) :    ~180,000
cache-read tokens            : 11,670,000

I had streamed 11.7 million cached tokens through the model that window and the bar barely moved — because only the ~180K of fresh input + output counted. Working backward, 5% = ~180K ⟹ a ceiling around 3.6M.

To be sure it's input+output and not something else, I checked each candidate metric against a hard fact: I have never once hit the 5-hour wall on Max5. So my heaviest-ever 5-hour window must come in under 100% on the correct metric:

What the meter might countImplied 5h ceilingMy heaviest windowFits "never hit it"?
Input + output only~3.6M2.43M (67%)✓ yes
… plus cache writes7.5M19.65M (262%)✗ no
… plus cache reads~233Mbillions✗ no

Only the first survives. The limit counts fresh input and output; cache reads and cache writes are effectively free against it. That's a real practical gift: a huge, warm context is cheap in dollars (cache reads bill at a tenth of input) and costs you almost nothing on the rate meter. What burns the meter is generating output and sending genuinely new, uncached input.

3. The ceilings — and the 5× checks out

I held the $20 plan before upgrading to Max5 ($100) on 12 June. Splitting my history at that moment and looking at peak 5-hour input+output:

Plan5-hour ceiling (in+out)My peak windowHit the wall?
$20~660K663Kyes — dead-on
Max5 ($100)~3.6M2.43M (67%)no — 2/3 of the way

On the $20 plan my busiest five hours topped out at 663K input+output and I hit the limit dead-on — so the ceiling is ~660K. Max5's ~3.6M is 5.4× that, almost exactly the advertised 5× rate-limit bump. And since my single heaviest window ever only reached 67% of the Max5 ceiling, it explains cleanly why I've never hit the 5-hour wall on the bigger plan.

The weekly cap is the real ceiling

The same calibration on the weekly bar: 6.9M input+output = 16% ⟹ ~43M per week. That's only about 12× the 5-hour ceiling, not the ~34 five-hour windows a week actually contains. In other words you can't run every five-hour window at the max all week — the weekly limit, not the 5-hour, is what ultimately paces sustained work.

What living at the weekly cap costs the provider

That cap sounds like it protects Anthropic. It mostly doesn't — because the expensive part, cache traffic, rides along uncapped. The limit throttles fresh input+output, but each of those tokens drags a large cached context with it, and that's where the cost actually is. Anchored to my live reading: reaching just this week's 16% has already generated about $1,044 of reference value (~$37–186 of real compute). Scaled to the full cap, and to a user who lives there every week:

A user who consistently maxes the weekly limit costs Anthropic an estimated $1,000–5,000 a month in raw compute against the $100 they pay — a structural four-figure monthly loss, on roughly $28,000 of API-equivalent value. And the limit doesn't prevent it: it caps throughput, not the cache-driven serving cost underneath. That's the real reason these caps exist at all — and why they keep tightening.

4. The Fable subplot

The cleanest way to see the limit bite is to follow my Fable 5 usage — the priciest, highest-throughput model I had. Fable shows up in my ledger for exactly three days: 10–12 June, about 3M new tokens and ~$245 of reference value per day, then nothing. The last Fable turn is stamped 12 June, 5:06 PM — because at 5:21 that afternoon, two hours after I upgraded, Fable was pulled (added to the export-ban list) and simply vanished from my options.

Those three Fable days are exactly when I kept slamming into the $20 five-hour wall: a high-output model against a ~660K ceiling fills it fast. The upgrade handed me 5× the room two hours before the model it would've fed disappeared anyway — timing I still find funny.

5. How I worked it out

No documentation needed — just the ledger and the percentage bar. Read the bar at a known moment, measure the input+output tokens consumed in that exact window from the ledger, and divide: tokens ÷ percentage = ceiling. Then sanity-check against an independent fact (the windows I did and didn't hit), and confirm the plan ratio lands near 5×. Three numbers, cross-checked, and the meter gives up its rules.
